01Why "Boring" Is Real (and Not Just a TikTok Thing)

Vertical SaaS — software built deep for one industry — is genuinely outpacing generic "horizontal" software, growing roughly ~32%/yr vs ~12%.1 The "unsexy" end of it has become a real prize: bootstrapped boring-software businesses command 7–10x ARR on exit, and PE is buying them aggressively (73 vertical-SaaS deals worth $15.3B in a single quarter).1 The structural reasons it works: the product is mission-critical, not convenient, so retention is high; the buyer pool is small enough that incumbents ignore it; and your accumulated knowledge of the workflow becomes a moat as raw AI gets commoditized.1

The best candidates share three traits: (1) currently run on paper, spreadsheets, or ancient on-prem tools; (2) a recurring, expensive pain; (3) a market big enough to matter but too small to attract big players.1 Analog-heavy fields — trades, healthcare-adjacent, agriculture, construction, compliance, logistics — are where this is hottest.1

Why it fits you specifically: no technical buyer to grill you, no "build it and they'll come" if you pick a niche you can reach, AI-buildable v1, designable to be low-maintenance, and a model serious investors respect. It routes around both of your fears. EST

02The Playbook — Find → Validate → Build → Sell

The order matters enormously. Most failures come from doing step 3 before steps 1–2. You build last, not first.

Step 1 · Find through access, not brainstorming

Start from who you can reach, then look for pain

Don't start with "what's a good idea." Start with "what unsexy worlds do I have a way into?" (§05 is the worksheet for this.) For each, go look for the recurring, expensive, manual task everyone hates. The niche finds you through a door you already have.

Step 2 · Validate the problem before you love it

10–15 problem interviews. Don't pitch — ask.

Find 10–15 people in the niche and ask about their current workflow and what they've tried — never pitch your solution.4 Lurk in their Facebook groups, subreddits, forums, trade-association threads for two weeks first.4 The gold-standard validation is a pre-sale: offer a discount to anyone who'll put money down now for something you ship in 30–60 days. If they won't pay, the pain isn't real enough.4 This is the same rigor you used to kill your overfit backtests — apply it here.

Step 3 · Build the smallest thing that removes the pain

Boring on purpose. One workflow, done right.

Solve the one painful task — not a platform. Rudimentary-but-reliable beats fancy-but-fragile in these markets. This is where your AI-assisted building is a genuine edge: you can ship a working v1 in a summer that a non-builder competitor can't. Design for low support load from day one (async value, few high-touch clients) so it doesn't follow you into your ND semester.

Step 4 · Sell as a business outcome, through trust

These buyers don't shop online — they're referred

Non-technical, older buyers buy problem-solving, not software, and they buy from people/channels they already trust — trade associations, resellers, industry advisors, word of mouth.3 Lead with the business-value math ("this saves you X hours / $Y a month"); buyers who get a value analysis are 3× more likely to move and 5× more likely to pay a premium.3 Invest heavily in hand-held onboarding — it's how you beat your trust gap as an 18-year-old.3

03The Niche Scorecard

Score any candidate niche 1–5 on each line. Multiply by the weight. But first check the two kill-switches — if either fails, the total doesn't matter.

⛔ Auto-kill switches (score ≤2 on either = walk away, regardless of total):
  • Access — do you have a real way to reach & understand these buyers? No door = no business, no matter how good the idea.
  • Acute recurring pain — is this a weekly/monthly bleed that costs them real time or money? A "nice to have" never converts older buyers.

04Honest Economics (the reality check)

Be the skeptic you were with your backtests. The model is real; the TikTok numbers are the new "6x backtest."

RealityThe honest number
When the first paying customer shows upOften week 12–18, not week 2. One case: 14 customers @ $29 = ~$406/mo by month 6.2
"Successful" no-audience trajectory EST~M1 $0.5–2k · M3 $2–10k · M6 $5–25k · M12 $10–50k — these are the winners' ranges, not the average.2

How most actually do70% of micro-SaaS make under $1,000/mo.2
#1 cause of deathNo market need — ~40–42% of failures. (This is why steps 1–2 come before building.)2
Churn realitySMB churn runs ~8.2%/mo (brutal) vs ~1% enterprise; >5%/mo logo churn = no product-market fit.2
Early revenue is jumpy50–200% month-swings at 0–3 mo (tiny customer counts) — don't read signal into noise.2
The upside if it works70%+ profit margins bootstrapped2; exits at 7–10x ARR, PE actively buying.1
Translation: this is a 12–24 month game to real money, not a summer. What a summer can realistically produce: a validated niche, a shipped v1, and your first few paying customers (~$300–1,500/mo EST) — which is a genuine win and a real foundation, even though it's a fraction of the TikTok flex. The high-churn SMB reality is also why niche + stickiness matter so much.

06Your Next 2 Weeks (a concrete sprint)

  1. Days 1–2: Fill in §05. Get 3–5 candidate worlds you can reach.
  2. Days 3–4: Run each through the §03 scorecard. Kill anything failing a kill-switch. Keep the top 2–3.
  3. Days 5–10: For your top niches, lurk their forums/FB groups/subreddits and book 10–15 problem interviews. Don't pitch. Hunt for the one expensive, recurring, manual task.4
  4. Days 11–14: Pick the single sharpest pain. Try a pre-sale — will anyone put money down for a 30–60 day delivery? That answer, not your enthusiasm, decides whether you build.4
Come back to me with: your filled-in access map and the 2–3 niches that survived the scorecard. We'll pressure-test them together, then design the smallest v1 and a build plan that fits the summer and stays low-maintenance once you're at ND. Don't write any code until a niche clears the kill-switches and someone signals they'd pay.
