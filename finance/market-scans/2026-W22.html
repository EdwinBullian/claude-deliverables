Top Moves This Week

1. Memory Joins the Trillion-Dollar Club (May 26–27)

Micron Technology briefly topped a $1T market cap on Tuesday, closing near $891 after a 19% single-day surge sparked by a UBS upgrade that tripled the firm's price target from $535 to $1,625 — now the highest on the Street. On Wednesday, SK Hynix followed Micron into the $1T club, with the Korean memory maker rising 9.3% (12-month gain >1,000%). The proximate cause is supply: Micron's entire 2026 HBM allocation is sold out, HBM4 is in production, and hyperscalers are now competing for memory the way they competed for GPUs eighteen months ago. This is the AI capex cycle widening — from compute (NVDA/AMD) → networking (CSCO) → memory (MU/SK Hynix) — within a single year. Five quarters ago Micron was a $50B cyclical commodity name. Today it's a $1T platform-AI bet.

2. Nvidia Q1 FY27 Print: "Demand Has Gone Parabolic" (May 20 result, traded through this week)

NVDA reported $81.6B in revenue (+85% YoY) versus a $79.2B consensus, with Data Center alone hitting $75.2B (+92% YoY). Non-GAAP EPS of $1.87 beat by 5.4%. Two capital-return surprises landed alongside the print: an $80B incremental buyback authorization and a 25x dividend hike ($0.01 → $0.25). Jensen's "Agentic AI has arrived" framing on the call did most of the work for the bull case. The setup we flagged in W21 (3-day equity selloff into the print compressing the bar) played out — the beat was clean, the guide was strong, and the entire AI infrastructure complex re-rated higher through this week.

3. S&P 500 + Nasdaq Hit Fresh Intraday All-Time Highs (Tue May 26)

The S&P closed at 7,519.12 (+0.61%), Nasdaq 26,656.18 (+1.19%). The Dow lagged (–0.23%). Mega-cap tech is leading; rate-sensitive industrials are not. The breadth divergence matters: this is a momentum-style rally concentrated in AI infrastructure, not a broad bull market. By Wednesday, chip stocks pulled back as Iran-deal optimism let some air out of the haven-energy trade and rotated money intraday.

4. SpaceX S-1 — The Largest IPO in History Is Live (Filed May 20)

SpaceX filed its public S-1 with the SEC on May 20, with a $75B raise — the largest IPO in history. Filing-stated valuation differs across coverage ($275B reported in some filings vs. a $1.75T–$2T+ target valuation in the roadshow framing), reflecting the gap between the cash-equity slice and the implied enterprise value. Roadshow begins June 8; pricing expected mid-to-late June. The $28.5T TAM ("multiplanetary economy") framing in the S-1 is doing the same conceptual work that Palantir's "ontology" framing did three years ago — a category-definition move designed to defend a premium even after the optionality math gets challenged. Eddie's watchlist has SpaceX flagged High; the thesis-build window is now.

5. Oil Down, Yields Down, Risk-On (May 22–27)

Oil dropped sharply on US-Iran framework-deal optimism. WTI ~$93, Brent ~$99 — both down >16% for May. Iran-deal contours include a 60-day ceasefire extension and a gradual Hormuz reopening; Iran's frozen assets and shipping guarantees remain the open issues. The 10Y Treasury yield pulled back to ~4.48% from its May 19 peak near 4.66%. The macro setup that crushed equities for three weeks (oil spike → yield spike → equity drawdown) has reversed cleanly. The question is whether this is a sustainable easing or a counter-trend bounce inside a still-broken cycle.

Counter-Intuitive Take

The memory rally is the AI cycle's first deflationary signal — and the market is celebrating it as the opposite.

Five consecutive Finance theses (PLTR, CRWD, AMD, NET, DBX) have landed on the same conclusion: best-in-class AI/data-infra companies trade at multiples that imply optionality DCF math can't justify. The standard read is that the market is pricing platform optionality the model can't see. The standard counterargument is that the market is wrong.

There's a third reading. Memory hitting $1T market caps in 48 hours — Micron and SK Hynix, both — is not a bullish capex signal. It's the late-cycle signal in every commodity-semiconductor boom I've read about: when the bottleneck moves down the stack to the most undifferentiated, most-cyclical layer, the cycle is closer to its top than its middle. HBM is real-world commodity capacity. It is fungible across customers. Capacity additions take 18–24 months. The 2021 DRAM peak looked exactly like this — the print before the peak was a UBS-style "200% upside" upgrade as supply tightness reached its visible maximum.

Here's the catch: the AI compute layer (NVDA) and the AI memory layer (MU/SK Hynix) cannot both be priced as platform-quality earnings. One is a platform with pricing power and a software moat — that's the NVDA case the market is making. The other is a fungible component whose moat is fab geometry. When the market re-rates both to platform multiples simultaneously, it's saying it can't tell them apart. That confusion historically resolves the same way: the platform name holds; the commodity name re-rates back to mid-cycle multiples; and the equity-market-cap loss from MU's re-rating is large enough to wipe out 6–12 months of S&P gains.

The reason this looks bullish instead of bearish: hyperscaler capex guidance is real, NVDA's parabolic-demand framing is real, OpenRouter at 100T tokens/month is real. Demand is not the problem. The problem is that the market has now decided that every layer of the AI stack deserves platform multiples, and that's the conviction that doesn't survive contact with the first capacity build-out cycle. UBS at $1,625 on Micron is the loudest bell I've seen in any sector in months — it's not the top, but it's the kind of price target that gets quoted in retrospectives.

The trade is not to short the AI cycle. The trade is to recognize that the commodity layer of the cycle is the part that breaks first, and to weight that layer down in any portfolio framing — even when it looks like the highest-momentum part of the trade. The PLTR/CRWD/AMD/NET/DBX thesis pattern was right about the gap. The Micron $1T print is the cleanest evidence yet that the gap is going to close from the top, not from the bottom.
