Two Launches, One Morning: SpaceX Hits the Pad and the Tape

Friday, June 12, 2026 · intraday snapshot (≈midday ET) · for learning, not investment advice

The setup. SpaceX ran two launches today — a Falcon 9 carrying 29 Starlink satellites off Cape Canaveral at dawn, and the one Wall Street cared about: its Nasdaq trading debut under ticker SPCX following the largest IPO in history. The rocket was routine. The stock is the story — and it's the lens for reading the whole tape today.

BlackRock alone reportedly placed a $5B order. A new 2× leveraged single-stock ETF (SPCL) launched the same day to give traders amplified exposure — a tell of how speculative the demand is. The valuation prices in years of flawless execution from day one: launch + Starlink is the profitable core, while the AI-compute/xAI fusion and next-gen bets carrying much of the premium are still losing money.

Bull framing: ARK's Brett Winton floats revenue reaching ~$400B by 2030 on Starlink scale + Starship economics. If you believe that, ~100× trailing collapses fast.
Bear framing: Steve Eisman calls $1.77T "crazy"; value managers note that mega-cap debuts priced for perfection have a long history of sharp de-ratings on the first stumble. At 100× sales there's no margin for error.

How I'd think about moves from here

Framing this like an analyst would — not telling you to buy or sell anything:

  • Don't chase the day-one SPCX pop. Mega-cap IPOs that open hot are the textbook case of buying other people's exit liquidity. The asymmetry on day one is bad: limited float, retail FOMO, and a 100× sales anchor mean the easy money was made by pre-IPO holders. Patience costs you little here.
  • Let the float and lockups breathe. The cleaner entry — if you ever wanted one — tends to come weeks later when the initial supply/demand imbalance normalizes and the first real earnings print sets a fair base. Watch how it digests, not how it opens.
  • The sympathy names are the second-order trade, and it's already crowded. SPCE +13% on no real news is sentiment, not fundamentals. If you're studying these, separate the ones with their own catalysts (RKLB, ASTS) from pure beta (SPCE, LUNR) — that distinction is the actual analytical work.
  • SPCL (the 2× ETF) is a volatility gauge, not a position. Leveraged single-stock ETFs decay; useful to watch as a sentiment thermometer, dangerous to hold.
  • Map the supply chain, not the logo. The durable VC/analyst question isn't "is SpaceX cheap" — it's which public suppliers, ground-station, satellite-component, and launch-adjacent names get pulled along as the sector's cost of capital drops. That's where a real thesis would live.
Bottom line: Today is a sentiment event, not a fundamentals event. The index is flat; the action is one name and its halo. The disciplined move is to watch and learn the price discovery on SPCX, note where valuation skeptics vs. growth bulls draw their lines, and resist the day-one chase. The interesting long-term work is the supply-chain map, not the ticker everyone's staring at.
