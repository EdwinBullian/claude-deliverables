1. Company Overview

Ford is a 122-year-old American automaker reorganized into three operating segments plus a captive finance arm — and that reorganization is the most important thing to understand about the equity. Management split the business into Ford Blue (legacy ICE — F-Series, Bronco, Explorer, Expedition), Ford Pro (commercial vehicles, fleet, software and service), and Ford Model e (EVs), with Ford Credit financing it all. The point was to force the market to value a highly profitable truck-and-commercial business separately from a deeply loss-making EV start-up.

The 2025 results made the logic clear. Ford posted record revenue of $187.3B but a GAAP net loss of $8.2B — its worst since the financial crisis. The loss was not operational: it was driven by ~$10.7B of Q4 Model e impairments and EV program cancellations (part of a broader ~$19.5B EV-related charge) as Ford wrote down assets after concluding its EV roadmap was uneconomic. Strip the special items out and adjusted EBIT was still +$6.8B (3.6% margin), the business generated $21.3B of operating cash flow and $3.5B of adjusted FCF, and Ford Credit earned $2.6B of EBT (+55%).

The franchise underneath is genuinely good in two of three legs: Ford Pro did >$66B revenue at $6.8B EBIT (double-digit margin, growing high-margin software); Ford Blue earned $3.0B EBIT on ~$101B revenue on the F-Series; Model e lost $4.8B. The entire debate is whether Pro and Blue are worth more than the ~$56B equity value once you stop letting Model e define the story.

2. Industry Position

Ford sits in the most contested tier of the industry: a high-volume, mass-market legacy OEM defending profit pools against Tesla and Chinese EV entrants on one side and GM, Stellantis, Toyota, and Honda on the other. Its durable advantages are narrow but real — the F-Series truck franchise (multi-decade pricing power), Ford Pro's entrenched fleet relationships (high switching costs once a fleet standardizes on Ford service and telematics), and the Blue Oval brand and dealer network. Its structural disadvantages are equally real — autos are capital-intensive ($9.5–10.5B capex in 2026 alone), cyclical, unionized, and tariff-exposed.

The 2026 read is that Ford is making the right defensive moves: after the EV write-down it is repivoting to a low-cost UEV platform for affordable, scalable EVs rather than chasing Tesla at the high end. Ford Pro is the genuinely differentiated asset — commercial customers are stickier and less price-sensitive than retail, and the software layer (879,000 paid subscriptions, +30% YoY) earns SaaS-like incremental margins. Ford's ~5% consolidated adjusted EBIT margin trails Toyota (~10%) and GM (~6.5%) but beats a weak Stellantis — squarely mid-pack, which is where its ~11x P/E sits.

3. The Cases

Bull — $27.62 (+96%)

A "sum-of-the-parts finally gets recognized" story. Ford Pro keeps compounding high-margin software (subs past 2M), Blue holds truck pricing, Model e reaches breakeven by ~2028 on the cheaper UEV platform instead of bleeding $4–5B/yr, and tariffs ease. Through-cycle adjusted EBIT margin holds ~6% and the market re-rates a de-risked, EV-loss-free Ford toward ~6x EV/EBIT. DCF: WACC 10.0%, terminal growth 2.0%, FY31E revenue ~$220B. Catalysts: Pro software margin inflection, a credible Model e breakeven date, tariff relief, buyback.

Base — $17.13 (+22%)

Slow, unglamorous normalization. Revenue grows ~1.7%/yr; consolidated adjusted EBIT margin recovers from 3.6% toward ~5.3% as Pro stays strong and Model e losses shrink but stay negative; capex ~5.8% of revenue keeps FCF conversion modest. This extends management's own 2026 framework: adjusted EBIT $8.5–10.5B (recently raised), adjusted FCF $5–6B, Pro $6.5–7.5B, Blue $4.0–4.5B, Model e −$4.0–4.5B, Ford Credit ~$2.5B. Discounted at a cyclical-appropriate 12% WACC, ~$17 — with the 4%+ dividend doing real work.

Bear — $9.97 (-29%)

The cycle, plus the value trap. US demand rolls over, pricing/mix reverse, Model e losses prove stubborn, tariffs escalate (Q1'26 margins were flattered by a $1.3B one-time IEEPA tariff benefit that does not recur). Margin compresses to 3.5–4%, the market applies a 13.5% discount rate, and in a true downturn Ford trims the dividend — removing the floor. Lands near $10, the 52-week low. Not a tail: Ford has cut its dividend in every serious modern recession.

The methods corroborate: the DCF base ($17.13) lands almost exactly on the comps EV/Rev-implied price ($17.18), while EV/EBIT pins Ford at roughly fair value. The most useful number is the reverse DCF: at $14.06 the market implies a through-cycle adjusted EBIT margin of only ~4.35% — below 2025's depressed 3.6% and well under the 5.3% base. The market is pricing structural decline; the investable question is whether Ford can hold a mid-5s margin through the cycle.

Modeling note (where beginners get autos wrong): the EV→equity bridge uses automotive net cash of ~+$9B (~$29B automotive cash less ~$20B automotive debt), not the $156B consolidated total debt on the balance sheet. Most of that debt sits inside Ford Credit, matched against finance receivables — self-funding, and it would massively overstate enterprise value if naively subtracted. The same adjustment was applied to GM, Toyota, and Stellantis in the comps.

5. Key Risks

Cyclicality. Autos are a leveraged bet on the consumer and the rate cycle; a recession compresses volume, price, and mix at once.
Tariffs / policy. FY25 carried ~$2.0B of tariff headwinds; Q1'26 was flattered by a $1.3B non-recurring benefit. Policy is the near-term EBIT swing factor.
EV cash burn. Model e is guided to another $4.0–4.5B loss in 2026. The UEV platform is a bet, not a result.
Dividend durability. The ~4.3% yield is core to the total-return case and the downside floor — and it is the first thing Ford cuts in a downturn.
Value-trap pattern. Ford has looked statistically cheap for a decade and repeatedly failed to re-rate. Cheap is not a catalyst.
Captive-finance opacity. Ford Credit's $150B+ balance sheet adds credit and funding risk easy to underappreciate from headline numbers.

6. Sector Takeaway

Ford is the watchlist's legacy-auto value anchor — the opposite of the AI/tech-infrastructure theses of recent weeks. The edge is valuation discipline, not growth: you earn by buying through-cycle earnings power at a discount and collecting the dividend, not by riding a re-rating. The DCF and comps bracket Ford at $14–18 — modestly cheap to fairly valued — with ~+95% optionality if Pro/software scale and Model e stops bleeding, against ~-30% downside if the cycle turns. That asymmetry plus a 4%+ yield supports a Medium-conviction "accumulate on weakness" stance: build in the $12–13 zone (yield >4.6%), target ~$18, trim above $24. Not a high-conviction buy at $14.06 — the margin of safety is thin and the value-trap history is real — but a defensible, income-supported holding.
