Snapshot

Anduril is the defining company of the defense-technology wave — a vertically integrated builder of autonomous weapons systems (drones, counter-drone systems, collaborative combat aircraft) wrapped around Lattice, its AI command-and-control software. In eleven months it roughly doubled its private valuation, closing a $5B Series H at ~$61B (Thrive Capital + Andreessen Horowitz) in May 2026, up from a $30B Series G ($2.5B) in June 2025. Revenue is compounding violently — $1.0B (2024) → $2.1B (2025A, +110%) → $4.3B (2026E guide, +105%) — while the company deliberately runs a ~$1.2B operating loss in 2026 to build Arsenal-1, its megafactory in Ohio.

The business is genuinely exceptional. The valuation is where the debate lives. My DCF and comps both land in the $30–45 normalized range — a ~60% discount to the $61B mark. That gap is not a claim that Anduril is a bad company; it is a measurement of how much future the Series H price already capitalizes.

Company Overview

Founded in 2017 by Palmer Luckey (Oculus) and a team from Palantir, Anduril rejected the cost-plus model that governs the traditional defense primes. Instead it funds its own R&D, builds products speculatively, and sells finished autonomous systems — betting that the Pentagon increasingly wants software-defined, mass-producible, attritable hardware rather than exquisite, decade-long platforms. Its portfolio spans Ghost and Anvil drones, the Roadrunner interceptor, Barracuda cruise missiles, Sentry surveillance towers, Dive-LD undersea vehicles, the Fury / YFQ-44A collaborative combat aircraft (CCA), and Lattice, the AI software layer tying sensors and effectors into a single autonomous kill web.

The 2025–2026 contract cadence is the real story:

  • $20B U.S. Army enterprise contract ceiling (10-year, March 2026) — consolidating 120+ procurement actions into one framework (ceiling, not obligated).
  • Air Force CCA production award (June 17, 2026) for the YFQ-44A Fury — prototype to full-rate manufacturing, alongside General Atomics.
  • Hypersonics — part of a DoD agreement to buy 10,000+ low-cost hypersonic missiles over three years (with CoAspire, Leidos, Zone 5).
  • $642M USMC counter-drone program and a $250M Pentagon counter-UAS award.
  • CBP border surveillance — 200 additional Sentry Towers (June 12, 2026).

Arsenal-1, the Ohio hyperscale weapons factory, began Fury production on March 23, 2026 — months ahead of the July target — with first aircraft expected summer 2026 and Fury, Roadrunner, Barracuda plus a classified platform in production by year-end. Campus spend exceeds $900M. CEO Brian Schimpf has said the company plans to stay private and views an IPO as “a distraction.” This is a pure pre-IPO / secondary-market name.

Industry Position & Competitive Dynamics

Anduril sits at the intersection of three tailwinds: (1) great-power competition re-inflating defense budgets; (2) a procurement-reform push (OTAs, rapid acquisition, attritable-mass doctrine) that favors commercial-style vendors; and (3) the maturation of edge AI/autonomy. Its competitive set is barbell-shaped: primes (Lockheed, GD, RTX, Leidos) trade at 1.4–1.8x forward revenue; software-defense and pure-autonomy names (Palantir ~38x; Kratos/AeroVironment ~4–5x; private Shield AI ~24x) trade far higher. Anduril's whole thesis is that it is the first company to be both — a prime's hardware scale with a software company's margins. If true, it deserves a software multiple; if it's a fast-growing hardware manufacturer with a good UI, it deserves a Kratos multiple. The ~30x gap between those worlds is the entire valuation argument. Its durable edge: breadth + Lattice as a platform — a genuine network effect as more systems are fielded.

Bull / Base / Bear

Bull — “The Software-Defined Prime” (implied ~$82)

Arsenal-1 hyperscales autonomous production; the $20B Army ceiling, CCA, and hypersonics convert into obligated revenue at software-like incremental margins; Lattice becomes the de-facto OS for allied autonomy. Revenue compounds >50% to ~$26B by 2031 at a 23% EBIT margin. Catalysts: Arsenal-1 first-article deliveries (2H26), added CCA lots, AUKUS/NATO adoption, an eventual IPO re-rate. Even this case is roughly the current mark — the bull is the price, not upside to it.

Base — “Great Company, Priced Ahead” (implied ~$29)

Anduril hits the $4.3B 2026 guide, scales to ~$20B by 2031 (~45% CAGR), and reaches a 19% steady-state margin as Arsenal-1 volume absorbs fixed cost. An excellent operating outcome — and still a −71% gap, because the mark already capitalizes the bull.

Bear — “Autonomy Is Hard, Margins Are Hardware” (implied ~$10)

Program timing slips, procurement reform stalls or reverses, and autonomous-systems economics stay hardware-like (price-to-win pressure, content cost). Revenue reaches only ~$10B by 2031; cash burn extends and dilutes. The equity is worth roughly the balance sheet.

Key Risks (ranked by probability × impact)

1. Valuation / entry risk (High × High). At $61B the mark prices ~$87B of 2031 revenue. Even flawless execution implies a large markdown. The dominant risk and the reason for a pass at the mark.
2. Margin-structure risk (High × High). The bull rests on hardware achieving software-like incremental margins — unproven at scale. A ~$1.2B 2026 operating loss is the starting point.
3. Government / policy concentration (Med × High). Revenue is overwhelmingly U.S. DoD. Budget cycles, CRs, procurement-reform reversal, or shifting priorities could stall the demand curve.
4. Execution / capex risk (Med × High). Arsenal-1 is a bet-the-company industrialization effort; delays hit revenue timing and the terminal-heavy DCF disproportionately.
5. Competition (Med × Med). Primes modernizing + funded single-product startups. Breadth/Lattice is the defense, but each product line is contested.
6. Liquidity / structure (High × Med). Private, no committed IPO, secondaries near/above the mark — a correct view may be hard to express at an attractive entry.

Recommendation & Takeaway

Pass at the $61B mark. Constructive only via secondary below ~$40B EV (~$65/normalized share). Re-run on a filed S-1 (if the IPO stance changes) or a ~180-day post-lockup window. Analysis, not advice.

What Anduril teaches (first non-AI-lab name after nine straight AI/tech theses):

  • Two-multiple companies are where the money and the mistakes are. Anduril is priced as software but operates hardware. Decide which multiple is right — don't split the difference. The mark assumes the software multiple.
  • When DCF and comps agree, respect it. In the AI-lab theses, rich private marks were echoed by rich public comps, so the methods diverged. Here, public defense comps are cheap, so DCF (−62%) and comps (−61%) point to the same conclusion — a higher-conviction signal.
  • “Great company” and “great investment” are orthogonal. Anduril may be the defense company of a generation and a poor entry at $61B. Separating business quality from price paid is the core VC/growth habit.

Bottom line: Extraordinary business, category-defining founder-led execution, real network effects — bought at a price that already assumes most of it works.
