1. Company Overview

Palantir builds enterprise AI and data orchestration software across two core products — Foundry (commercial data integration/analytics) and Gotham (classified government/defense) — plus AIP (AI Platform), launched 2023, which connects LLMs to enterprise data through Foundry's ontology-first architecture. Founded 2003 by Peter Thiel et al., CIA-backed origin, now the largest pure-play AI software company in the S&P 500.

FY2025 actuals: Revenue $4.46B (+53% YoY) | Gov/Commercial mix 55%/45% | Adjusted Op Margin ~40% | FCF ~$1.6B | $10B / 10-year US Army TITAN/Vantage contract (Aug 2025). FY2026 guidance: Revenue $7.18–7.20B (+61%) | Q1 2026: $1.53B revenue, $870M adj. op income (57% Q1 adj op margin guide). US Commercial revenue: +115% YoY.

2. Industry Position

Palantir sits at the intersection of data infrastructure (Snowflake, Databricks), application workflow (Salesforce, ServiceNow), and AI/LLM (OpenAI, Anthropic). AIP's pitch: a fourth layer — orchestration — connecting all three without multi-year integration nightmares. Two structural advantages: ontology-first architecture (semantic data model that routes LLM calls to actual business objects, not raw text) and Forward Deployed Engineers (FDEs) that create switching costs. Contested ground: MSFT (Fabric + Copilot Studio), Salesforce (Agentforce), hyperscalers, and Databricks (pre-IPO) all converging on the orchestration layer.

AIP becomes the de facto enterprise AI orchestration layer. Commercial bookings sustain Q4 2025's 137% YoY pace through 2027 before normalizing. $10B Army contract expands to allied governments (UK MOD, Australia, Five Eyes). 🟡 Base Case — $33 implied (DCF)

Microsoft/Salesforce enter orchestration credibly with bundled offerings. Multiple normalizes to mature SaaS peers (~12–15x rev) on half the bull-case revenue growth. FY31 endpoint: ~$16B revenue, ~32% steady-state op margin.

Sector Takeaway — What PLTR Teaches

DCF is a discipline, not an answer for stocks like this. Every responsible analyst from 2023–today has had a DCF saying PLTR is overvalued. The stock is up ~10x over that window. The discipline is to do both: build the DCF, and be honest that it doesn't capture the upside narrative — without abandoning the discipline that says fundamentals eventually matter.

Reverse-DCF is the right framing for category-of-one stocks. When forward-modeling fails to bridge to spot, ask: what would have to be true? That answer ("$68B FY31") is concrete enough to interrogate quarterly — each earnings becomes a referendum on whether the implied path is on track.

The "platform vs. point solution" question is the central tech investing question of this decade. PLTR's bull case is platform-becomes-default. Its bear case is platform-gets-bundled. Every enterprise software bet through 2030 comes back to this.
