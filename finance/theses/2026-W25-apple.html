Apple is the highest-quality consumer franchise on the planet, and right now that is precisely the problem. After an iPhone 17 supercycle, a record holiday quarter, and Services compounding into a ~$125B+ run-rate, the stock has re-rated to ~$298 (~$4.4 trillion) — roughly 34x forward and ~40x trailing earnings, above Apple's long-run ~26–30x range. The business deserves a premium. It does not, on any defensible cash-flow basis, deserve this premium.

A DCF on generous assumptions (7% revenue CAGR to FY31, ~33% EBIT margins, 9.2% WACC, 3.25% terminal growth) yields a base case of $170 (−43%). The bull case still reaches only $272 (−9%); the bear is $97; probability-weighted fair value is ~$177 (−41%). The reverse DCF makes it concrete: $298 embeds ~15% revenue CAGR for six years — nearly doubling to ~$960B by FY31. Apple grew 6% last year.

This is a discipline call, not a short. Momentum, the buyback, and quality can sustain the price well past intrinsic value; the right posture is to admire the company, respect the tape, and let valuation come to you.

Company Overview

Apple sells premium hardware — iPhone, Mac, iPad, Watch, AirPods, Vision — wrapped in a software/services ecosystem locking in 2.35B+ active devices. Hardware (~75% of revenue, led by iPhone) drives the installed base and throws off cash but grows with the premium-device cycle. Services (App Store, iCloud, Apple Pay, advertising, AppleCare, the Google default payment) is a ~$125B+ run-rate business growing mid-teens at ~70%+ gross margins — the engine behind Apple's margin and multiple expansion.

FY2025 (ended Sept 27, 2025): $416B revenue (+6%), $112B net income (~27% net margin), ~$99B free cash flow, $100B+ returned to shareholders. Momentum into FY2026 is exceptional: Q1 FY26 was Apple's best quarter ever (~$144B); Q2 FY26 hit $111.2B (+17%) with iPhone $57.0B (+22%) and Services a record $31.0B (+16%), EPS $2.01 (+22%), and a June-quarter guide of +14–17%. The board added a $100B buyback and raised the dividend to $0.27.

Leadership transition: Tim Cook hands the CEO role to hardware chief John Ternus on September 1, 2026, becoming executive chairman. Orderly and long-telegraphed, but the most significant leadership change in over a decade — arriving as expectations sit at all-time highs.

Industry Position

Apple's moat — hardware/software integration, switching costs (iMessage, Health, Wallet), and durable pricing power — is among the widest in tech. But within the mega-cap cohort Apple is now the slowest grower. Microsoft (Azure ~40%), Alphabet (Cloud +63%), Amazon (AWS +28%), Meta, and Nvidia are riding the AI build-out, collectively committing $725B+ of 2026 capex. Apple is deliberately not a capex-heavy AI infrastructure player; Apple Intelligence is an on-device, ecosystem-retention strategy. Defensible — but it means Apple lacks the secular top-line accelerant the market is paying its peers for. The risk: an AI-era multiple awarded for a non-AI-era growth rate.

The Cases

Bull — $272 (−9%)

The supercycle has legs: iPhone 17/18 sustains double-digit hardware growth, Apple Intelligence triggers a multi-year upgrade wave across 2.35B devices, Services compounds past a $140B run-rate, and a new category (Vision, home, health) adds a fourth leg. ~9% revenue CAGR to ~$706B by FY31 at 35% EBIT margins, 8% WACC. Even here, intrinsic value is below spot — the most important fact in this thesis.

Base — $170 (−43%)

Most probable path. FY2026 +12% on the iPhone 17 cycle, then deceleration toward mid-single digits as hardware normalizes; Services remains the growth/margin engine. ~7% revenue CAGR to ~$623B, ~33% EBIT margins, 9.2% WACC, 3.25% terminal. A best-in-class business worth ~$170 — extraordinary franchise, irrational price.

Bear — $97 (−68%)

iPhone growth stalls on saturation and China share loss; an adverse Google antitrust remedy pressures the ~$20B+ search-default payment flowing into Services profit; AI/silicon spend weighs on margins. ~3% revenue CAGR, margins toward 29%, multiple de-rates to a GDP-grower's ~18–20x. The scenario the current price most ignores.

Valuation

DCF (primary) — probability-weighted (25/50/25) fair value ~$177, or −41% vs spot. Terminal value carries 70–80% of PV in every scenario — appropriate for a durable franchise, but a reminder the value rests on long-dated assumptions.

Reverse DCF (the punchline) — $298 embeds ~15% revenue CAGR for six years (~2.3x to ~$960B by FY31) plus sustained ~35% margins. A heroic ask for a $416B hardware-led business that grew 6% last year.

Comps (secondary) — Apple's ~9.3x EV/FY26E revenue sits right at the peer median, implying ~$293 (−2%): "fairly valued versus an already-expensive cohort." But Apple is the slowest grower in the set; peers' multiples are earned by 17–50% growth. The comps don't say Apple is cheap — they say the whole mega-cap complex is priced for perfection and Apple has the least growth to defend it. When relative and absolute disagree this sharply, the absolute number protects capital.

Key Risks (to the bearish-on-valuation view)

Momentum + buyback can overwhelm valuation. A $100B+ annual repurchase shrinking the float, plus passive flows, can hold the price far above fair value. Being early is indistinguishable from being wrong for a long time.
The supercycle could be structural. If Apple Intelligence durably re-accelerates upgrades and Services holds high-teens growth, the base case is too conservative.
Margin mix shift is real. Every point of Services mix lifts blended margin; the model may under-credit it.
Quality may warrant a lower discount rate. A ~7.5–8% WACC (used in the bull case) lifts intrinsic value meaningfully on its own.

Sector Takeaway & Recommendation

Apple is this quarter's cleanest example of a recurring mega-cap pattern: wonderful businesses re-rated to multiples that require the bull case to be the base case. The franchise is a 10; the stock at $298 is a 4. The disciplined call is to avoid or trim at spot and let valuation — not the company — come to you. Meaningful interest builds in the ~$190–210 zone, where the base-to-bull range offers an actual margin of safety.
