Bottom line: SpaceX is filing the largest IPO in history — ~$75B raised at a ~$1.75T target. It is three businesses in one: Starlink (the only profit center), Launch (a strategic moat run at a deliberate loss), and xAI (a $6.4B/yr cash furnace). A disciplined DCF lands ~94% below the IPO mark — the most extreme DCF-vs-market gap in six consecutive theses. The lesson: when the gap is this large, the DCF is the wrong primary tool. Use sum-of-the-parts. Even a generous SOTP struggles to clear ~$1.0–1.2T without heroic terminal assumptions. Admire the business; decline the IPO; reassess post-lockup.

1. Company Overview

The S-1 (CIK 0001181412) is the first real look inside a two-decade black box. 2025 consolidated: revenue $18.674B (+33% YoY), operating loss -$2.589B, adjusted EBITDA +$6.584B. Q1 2026 decelerated: revenue $4.694B (+15%), op loss -$1.943B, adj EBITDA $1.127B. The 2025 net loss of ~$4.94B (vs $791M profit in 2024) is explained entirely by one segment.

The investable truth: Starlink is the company. Launch is a moat and a cost center funding Starship. xAI dilutes earnings quality through a related-party merger. Any honest valuation must value the parts.

2. Industry Position & Competitive Dynamics

Launch: global near-monopoly; Falcon 9 reusability is a cost advantage no rival matches at scale; Starship is the next order-of-magnitude step. Starlink: the only consumer-scale LEO broadband constellation, vertically integrated with its own launch supply, expanding into direct-to-cell. Amazon Kuiper is the credible threat but years behind and does not own its launch stack. xAI: a second-tier frontier lab, price-taker on compute and talent, burning $6B+/yr with no disclosed profitability path. The S-1's cited $28.5T TAM is the tell — this is a story stock by design. The moat is real where it matters; the valuation is underwritten on the optionality.

The SOTP midpoint sits well below the ~$1.75T IPO ask in any disciplined build. The gap is "optionality" — Mars, full direct-to-cell TAM, Starship as a platform, defense.

Discipline check — DCF

A 6-year unlevered DCF (base: ~23% revenue CAGR to ~$66B FY31E, op margin 5%→25%, capex 30%→12% of revenue, WACC 11.7%, g 3.5%) yields a base ~$11/share (-94%); bull ~$24; bear ~$1. These look broken — and that is the lesson. A finite-horizon FCF model cannot value an infrastructure builder with negative FCF through ~2027E and a value harvest that arrives after the window. The DCF is a floor/discipline check: it quantifies how much "story" is in the price.

Cross-check — Comps

No true public comp exists. The space/satellite cohort (RKLB, ASTS, IRDM, VSAT, GSAT) trades at a peer-median ~10x EV/Revenue FY26E → only ~$240B EV on SpaceX's revenue. The 75th percentile (RKLB-like ~45x) implies ~$1.07T. Only AST SpaceMobile's ~240x — a pre-commercial optionality name — is rich enough to "justify" the IPO. SpaceX's own implied multiple is ~73x EV/Revenue FY26E, richer than every profitable peer.

4. Bull / Base / Bear

Bull — implied DCF ~$24; SOTP can clear the IPO. Starlink becomes the global broadband + direct-to-cell standard (~30%+ growth, 60%+ EBITDA margins); Starship reaches reusability, collapsing launch cost and unlocking defense/payload revenue; xAI inflects toward breakeven. Catalysts: Starship cadence milestones, Starlink unit-economics disclosure, defense awards, direct-to-cell carrier deals.
Base — implied DCF ~$11; SOTP ~$0.5–0.9T. Starlink scales but decelerates as Kuiper/direct-to-cell competition emerges; launch stays a break-even moat; Starship monetizes slowly; xAI losses moderate but persist. Excellent business, worth a great deal — but less than $1.75T on disciplined math. Likely drifts toward lockup expiries. Catalysts: first public quarters, capex trajectory.
Bear — implied DCF ~$1; SOTP ~$200B+. Starship slips again, capex never normalizes, xAI burn deepens and drags FCF negative for years, multiple compresses. The near-zero DCF is not a real floor — Starlink alone exceeds $100B — which is exactly why SOTP sets the true downside. Catalysts: Starship failure, xAI cash calls, key-person risk.

5. Key Risks (probability × impact)

1. Optionality re-rating (high × high). The IPO embeds decades of terminal execution; the likely path is a post-IPO drift lower as public markets demand cash flow.
2. xAI drag & related-party governance (high × med-high). Consolidating a $6.4B-loss lab via an insider merger degrades earnings quality; super-voting control limits minority recourse.
3. Starship execution (med × high). The launch-cost-revolution leg depends on a delay-prone vehicle.
4. Capex intensity / funding (med × high). Starlink replenishment + Starship + xAI compute are simultaneous cash demands; sustained negative FCF is the base case.
5. Competition (med × med). Amazon Kuiper (+ Globalstar for direct-to-cell) and Rocket Lab Neutron erode the monopoly premium over time.
6. Key-person risk (low-med × high). Musk is the strategy, fundraising, and narrative.

6. Takeaway — What SpaceX Teaches

This is the sixth consecutive thesis (PLTR, CRWD, AMD, NET, DBX, now SpaceX) where a disciplined DCF lands far below market — and the most extreme yet (-94%). The new lesson: when the gap is this large, the DCF is the wrong primary tool. For an infrastructure builder with back-end-loaded cash flows, switch to SOTP and scenario/optionality analysis; keep the DCF as a discipline check that measures how much "story" is in the price.

Entry discipline: SpaceX is genuinely extraordinary — that's not the question. The question is price. At ~$1.75T the IPO asks you to underwrite near-flawless multi-decade execution with no margin of safety. Admire the business, decline to chase the IPO, and size a position when the math and the price move closer to agreement — likely after lockups and real public disclosures.
