SaaS Metrics That Matter

Research Date: 2026-05-19 | Effort: 8 hrs | Queue Priority: #1 | Week 2026-W21

Topics: ARR, NRR/GRR, CAC/LTV, Rule of 40, Magic Number, RPO, Red Flags

Executive Summary

SaaS metrics are the language of tech-focused finance — IB, PE, and VC alike. A company growing ARR at 40% but hemorrhaging customers is fundamentally different from one growing 25% with 95%+ gross retention, even if their top-line numbers look similar in a given quarter. The key takeaway: NRR is the single most powerful predictor of valuation multiples — for every 1% increase in revenue retention, a SaaS company's value increases ~12% over five years. But NRR alone is incomplete without GRR, unit economics, and the Rule of 40.

1. ARR / MRR — The Revenue Foundation

What It Is & Why It Matters

ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) = annualized value of all active subscription contracts. MRR = ARR ÷ 12. These are the primary metrics for measuring SaaS size and trajectory — and they matter more than GAAP revenue because they isolate the subscription engine, the part of the business that compounds.

ARR = MRR × 12 Include: recurring subscription fees only Exclude: implementation fees, professional services, hardware, one-time items Company with $100M GAAP revenue + $70M ARR ($30M services) is fundamentally less valuable than $100M GAAP revenue + $95M ARR.

The MRR bridge: Sophisticated analysts decompose MRR rather than projecting aggregate growth: Net New MRR = New MRR + Expansion MRR − Contraction MRR − Churned MRR. This lets you model acquisition rates, expansion rates, and churn rates independently — the approach used by PE firms in SaaS diligence.

Where to find it: Non-GAAP metric disclosed in MD&A "Key Business Metrics" subsection (when disclosed at all). CrowdStrike, Snowflake, Datadog report explicitly. Others require backing into an ARR estimate by stripping services revenue from the income statement.

2026 benchmark: Median public SaaS ARR growth: 19–21% Top quartile: 40%+

2. NRR — The Single Most Powerful SaaS Metric

Net Revenue Retention

NRR measures how much revenue existing customers generate over time, including expansion, contraction, and churn. It answers: "If we stopped acquiring new customers entirely, would revenue grow or shrink?"

NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion MRR − Contraction MRR − Churned MRR) / Starting MRR × 100 Example: Start $50K MRR | Lose $1.5K churn + $500 downgrades | Gain $3K upsells NRR = ($50K − $1.5K − $500 + $3K) / $50K = 102%

Why NRR > 100% is transformative: An NRR of 120% means existing customers generate 20% more revenue annually without acquiring a single new customer. New customer acquisition stacks on top of organic expansion — closest thing to a "cheat code" in SaaS economics.

Where to find it: MD&A section. Different names: "dollar-based net retention rate" (CrowdStrike, Snowflake), "net revenue retention rate" (Datadog), "dollar-based net expansion rate" (Twilio). Always read the company's specific definition — methodologies vary.

2026 benchmark: Median public SaaS NRR: 101% Top quartile: 111%+ Elite (Snowflake peak): 130%+

3. GRR — The Hidden Truth Behind NRR

Gross Revenue Retention

GRR measures revenue retained from existing customers before counting expansion. It cannot exceed 100%. It reveals whether expansion revenue is masking a churn problem.

GRR = (Starting MRR − Contraction MRR − Churned MRR) / Starting MRR × 100 Company A: GRR 95%, NRR 115% → Healthy. Low churn, genuine expansion on top. Company B: GRR 75%, NRR 115% → Dangerous. Losing 25% of base annually, covering it with aggressive upselling. If expansion slows, churn becomes visible fast. Both have identical NRR. Company A is far more valuable.

This GRR/NRR distinction is one of the most important analytical insights in SaaS: a high NRR can mask a serious churn problem. PE and VC due diligence always asks for both.

2026 benchmark: Median GRR: 89% Top quartile: 95%+ Enterprise SaaS best-in-class: 95–98%

4. CAC, LTV & Unit Economics

Is Growth Economically Sustainable?

CAC = Total S&M Spend / New Customers Acquired LTV = (ARPA × Gross Margin) / Customer Churn Rate where ARPA = ARR / Total Customers LTV:CAC Ratio: <1:1 → Losing money on every customer 1–3:1 → Inefficient 3–5:1 → Healthy (target) >5:1 → Efficient or underinvesting in growth CAC Payback Period = CAC / (ARPA × Gross Margin / 12) Target: <18 months enterprise SaaS, <12 months SMB SaaS

Critical: Use fully-loaded S&M costs — salaries, commissions, SBC allocated to S&M, tools, paid media, events. Using only direct marketing spend understates true CAC significantly.

Why payback matters more than LTV:CAC in practice: A 4:1 LTV:CAC ratio looks great on paper, but if payback takes 30 months and you're burning $200K/month, you run out of cash before the economics materialize. Payback captures the timing dimension that LTV:CAC misses.

2026 benchmark: Public SaaS median LTV:CAC: 4.2:1 Private SaaS median: 3.1:1

5. Rule of 40

Growth-Profitability Balance

Rule of 40 Score = Revenue Growth Rate (%) + FCF Margin (%) A company growing 50% with -10% margins (score: 40) is as "healthy" as one growing 15% with 25% margins (score: 40).

But not all 40s are equal: Buyers pay more per point of growth than per point of margin. A score of 45 built on 35% growth + 10% margin is worth more than 5% growth + 40% margin. Growth creates optionality; margin preserves current value.

Evolving to "Rule of X": Score = (Growth Rate × 2) + FCF Margin. This reflects the empirical reality that markets assign a higher multiple per point of growth. Under this framework, a 25% grower with 10% margins (score: 60) significantly outranks a 10% grower with 30% margins (score: 50).

2026 benchmark: Median public SaaS: 28% Only 20% exceed 40 >40 companies: 10.7x EV/Rev avg Each +10 pts ≈ +1.1x EV/Rev

6. Magic Number & RPO

Sales Efficiency & Forward Visibility

Magic Number = [(Current Qtr Revenue − Prior Qtr Revenue) × 4] / Prior Qtr S&M Spend >1.0 → Efficient. Signal to increase investment. 0.5–1.0 → Acceptable. <0.5 → Inefficient. Investigate sales cycle, market fit, or spending. RPO (Remaining Performance Obligations) = all contracted revenue not yet recognized = Deferred Revenue (invoiced) + Unbilled Backlog (contracted, not invoiced) cRPO (current) = revenue expected in next 12 months → most actionable for forecasting Billings = Revenue + Change in Deferred Revenue Rising deferred revenue (billings > revenue) = customers prepaying → positive signal

RPO analytical use: If revenue is growing 25% but cRPO is growing 35%, the business is likely to accelerate. If cRPO growth lags revenue growth, deceleration may be ahead.

8. The Analyst's SaaS Evaluation Framework

Layer 1 — Revenue Quality: ARR? % of total revenue that's recurring? ARR growth rate?
Layer 2 — Retention Quality: NRR? GRR? Is expansion masking churn? How do metrics trend over time?
Layer 3 — Unit Economics: LTV:CAC ratio? CAC payback period? Magic Number?
Layer 4 — Growth-Profitability Balance: Rule of 40 score? Built on growth or margin? Trajectory?
Layer 5 — Forward Visibility: RPO growth? cRPO vs. revenue growth? Pipeline accelerating or decelerating?
Layer 6 — Red Flag Scan: GAAP/non-GAAP divergence? Customer concentration? GRR declining? Late filings?

Implications for Eddie

For IB recruiting and interviews: SaaS metrics questions are increasingly common in TMT banking interviews. Knowing formulas is table stakes — what separates candidates is understanding why NRR matters more than revenue growth for valuation, or explaining the GRR/NRR distinction with the "expansion masking churn" insight. The framework for finding metrics in 10-K filings is directly applicable to the analyst workflow.

For VC career goals: VC investors live and die by these metrics at the startup level. Early-stage companies don't file 10-Ks — you get this data from management decks and data rooms. But the analytical framework is identical. Understanding how NRR compounds, what a healthy LTV:CAC looks like, and when a Magic Number signals product-market fit vs. market saturation is core VC pattern recognition.
