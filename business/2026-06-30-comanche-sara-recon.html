Part 1 — On-Site Recon Playbook Do in 48h

A · The data-exit question Largely answered

This is what decides whether any AI/automation layer is buildable — and you've already confirmed the answer is yes: Comanche exports to Excel on a single button press. Comanche is closed third-party software (you won't modify it), so the layer reads its outputs — and the output path is wide open. Nail down the few remaining specifics:

  • Where does the export go? (most important remaining unknown) Press the button and watch: does it download/save a file to a folder on the machine, or email it somewhere? If it saves, note the exact folder path and filename pattern. If it emails, note the address. This determines how an automation later picks the file up.
  • What's in the one-button export vs. report-by-report? Is it a single workbook or per-report files? Export the manager's whole daily set and see what you get.
  • Is there scheduling? Ask IT/the rep whether exports can run automatically on a timer (a "Scheduler" or auto-export). If yes, automation can be fully hands-off; if no, someone clicks the button — still fine for v1.
  • Database underneath (nice-to-have): if easy, open Windows ODBC Data Sources and screenshot any DSNs (engine/version/server). Older Thai PMS often run on Sybase SQL Anywhere or MS SQL. Not urgent now that Excel export is confirmed, but useful if you ever want richer/real-time access.
  • Version: Help → About. Screenshot it.

Also confirmed: Comanche is role-gated by department — housekeeping/maid data isn't visible to front desk or kitchen, etc. Implication for anything you build: data is siloed by role, so a reporting layer that aggregates across departments (with permission) is doing something individual staff logins can't — that's part of the value. It also means you'll want a login with broad enough access to export the full picture.

B · Export real sample data (your build fuel)

For each item below, export to Excel/CSV and PDF, and save copies to a USB drive and OneDrive. This becomes your test set once you're home — you cannot get it later.

  • A full week of the daily reports the manager actually reads: manager flash / night-audit report, occupancy & RevPAR, room revenue, F&B daily sales, AR aging.
  • Arrivals / departures / in-house guest list.
  • 2–3 sample folios / invoices.
  • Month-end back-office output: P&L, trial balance, GL export.
  • Whatever data feeds the SARA dashboard (see Section D).
  • A guest-profile export (anonymize names if needed — don't carry PII you don't need).

C · Screenshot every screen

Walk each module menu and capture it: Front Office, POS, Inventory Control, Back Office/Accounting, Event Management, Reports. Critically, screenshot the full list of canned report names in the Reports module — that inventory tells you what's already automated vs. what staff still build by hand.

D · SARA — identity confirmed Resolved

Settled: SARA is S.A.R.A Operations Platform by DXM Solutions Pte Ltd (Singapore vendor, hotelservices.online) — a commercial cloud SaaS the hotel subscribes to, not custom software and not a Comanche module. It's the staff messaging + service-request/dispatch tool (the "professional LINE"), with a guest-request app. See Part 2 for the full profile. A couple of light things still worth capturing while you're there:

  • Confirm whether their SARA is wired to Comanche via its 2-way PMS integration (HTNG), or runs standalone. (Changes what data already flows between the two.)
  • Screenshot SARA's own Reports + dashboard screens — so you know exactly what request/ops analytics it already covers (and therefore what not to rebuild).
  • Note what people still send over LINE instead of SARA — you mentioned daily reports. Confirm: what exactly still goes through LINE, and who assembles it?

E · Staff pain interviews (Mom Test — past & concrete)

Talk to the GM, front-office manager, and the accountant. Ask about the last time, never the hypothetical. You're hunting for hours-per-week of manual work, not opinions.

  • "Walk me through the last daily report you built by hand — what did you copy, from which screen, into what?"
  • "How long did last month-end close take? What was the annoying part?"
  • "What number does the owner / head office ask for that's a pain to pull?"
  • "What do you re-type out of Comanche into Excel or email every week?"
  • "What does Comanche just not do that you wish it did?"

Get numbers: hours/week, who does it, how often. "3 hours every Monday building the weekly pack" is the kind of evidence that justifies building anything.

F · Commercials & constraints

Quick asks that decide the economics later: annual license/support cost, contract lock-in, who legally owns the data, last time it was upgraded, and whether the aunt would let you use a property as a design partner.

Part 2 — Strategic Brief

What Comanche is

Comanche International is a Thailand-based hospitality-IT company, founded 1996 and listed on Thailand's MAI exchange in 2016 (ticker COMAN). Its PMS is used by ~1,200 hotels and serviced apartments across ~19 countries, heavily concentrated in Thailand and Asia. It's a full-suite, on-premise (local client-server) system — which matches exactly what you described: it lives on their computers, not the cloud. Modules span Front Office (reservations, front desk, housekeeping, billing, night audit), Point of Sale, Back Office accounting (AP/AR/GL), Inventory Control, Event Management, plus a large library of third-party interfaces (channel managers, booking engines, door locks, passport scanners, CRM, yield management).

Strengths

  • One-button Excel export — confirmed on-site; data exit is trivial (this is the big one for any AI layer)
  • Deep, mature, genuinely integrated feature set (30 yrs of edge cases)
  • Built for the Thai/Asian market — local tax, compliance, language, on-the-ground support
  • Large interface library you'd otherwise have to rebuild from scratch
  • Cheap relative to Oracle OPERA; entrenched and sticky; runs locally/offline

Weaknesses

  • Dated client-server architecture; needs on-site servers + someone to maintain them
  • Reporting is canned operational reports, not flexible BI — exports are raw, not insight
  • Role-gated by department — no single view aggregates across the property
  • Little to no native AI; weak remote/mobile access
  • Thin public reputation — 0 verified reviews, no Hotel Tech "Certificate of Excellence"

What SARA actually is Confirmed

SARA is S.A.R.A Operations Platform by DXM Solutions Pte Ltd — a Singapore-based commercial cloud SaaS (hotelservices.online), listed on the Oracle Cloud Marketplace, marketing "400+ hotels and 50,000+ users." It's a subscription product the hotel buys — not custom-built for them, and not part of Comanche. Category: staff messaging + service-request dispatch — the "professional LINE" for live operations, with a guest-request app.

It does: department-routed task/request management (housekeeping, maintenance, room service, concierge, IT), auto-dispatch with auto-escalation when a request stalls, a real-time chat room per request (with images), room-inspection/engineering checklists, a guest app that auto-routes requests to the right department, ops/request reports, and a 2-way PMS integration over HTNG. Multi-language (11+ UI, 133 chat-translation). This maps exactly to what you observed: bellboy bag alerts, cleaning requests, guests asking for an iron/water through the app.

Strengths

  • Purpose-built real-time dispatch + escalation — genuinely useful, sticky
  • Mobile-first + guest app; cloud, no local install
  • Already does operational request analytics
  • Funded incumbent (400+ hotels) — credible, maintained

Weaknesses / gaps

  • Scoped to ops requests — does not touch financial / management reporting
  • Doesn't fully displace LINE — staff still use LINE for "big stuff" like daily reports
  • Generic "AI-powered" branding; not deep, hotel-specific intelligence

The three layers at the hotel — and the unowned seam

With SARA confirmed, the operating picture is clean. There are three layers, and the opportunity is the gap between them:

LayerToolOwns
1ComancheSystem of record — all money + ops data. Easy Excel export out.
2SARAReal-time ops dispatch/comms — live requests + guest app.
3LINEWhatever's left — including "the big stuff," i.e. daily report packs.

The seam = management reporting. Comanche emits raw Excel. SARA deliberately doesn't touch financial/management reporting. So the daily/weekly/monthly report packs are still assembled by hand and pushed through LINE. No one owns that layer — and it's fed by the one thing that's trivially available (Comanche's one-button Excel). That seam is your wedge.

You compete with neither incumbent. You sit in the gap they both ignore.

The call: do NOT replace Comanche or SARA Blunt

Replacing Comanche = building a PMS = the exact saturated trap Module 1 flagged. 30-year incumbent, 1,200 installs, local compliance, interface library you'd have to recreate. You'd lose, and it wouldn't prove the thesis.

Out-building SARA is the same mistake one layer up: it's a funded incumbent (400+ hotels) that already owns real-time dispatch and operational request analytics, and it's already branding "AI." Don't aim your AI play at ops tickets — that's contested ground. Both of these are shiny-object pulls; name them and kill them.

The thesis test: AI is only a durable edge where it multiplies something only you have. Rebuilding a PMS or a dispatch app multiplies nothing — anyone can build those. Owning the report seam, fed by data you have privileged access to, does.

The actual play: an AI reporting layer in the seam Thesis-positive

Don't change either system; read Comanche's Excel exports and produce the management reporting that currently goes through LINE by hand — the daily flash, the weekly pack, the month-end summary, the owner's ad-hoc questions. This is the same "AI-into-existing-workflow" track as your other session, and it's where your real advantages live:

  • Access — your aunt's hotels are a live design partner with real data and a warm intro. Strangers can't get that.
  • Speed — your agent OS turns an export into a polished, cross-department report pack faster than a vendor ships a feature.

That passes the thesis test: the edge multiplies access + iteration speed, not a commodity anyone can generate.
